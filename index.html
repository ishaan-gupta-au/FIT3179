Global Impact of Cattle on Greenhouse Gas Emissions

The Earth has experienced rapid climate change in the recent years. Though we may not often think about what we eat, food has a major carbon footprint, accounting for over a quarter of greenhouse gas emissions across the globe. We wish to explore this impact in further detail and determine how cattle impacts the environment.

Share of Global Greenhouse Gas Emissions From Food

The adjacent graph illustrates the global share of greenhouse gas emissions caused by food as at 2015. Of all the countries, China occupies the highest share of emissions, followed by Indonesia, the U.S.A, Brazil and India.

Carbon Emissions Per Kilogram of Food

Generally, animals release the most carbon emissions in the atmosphere, with beef herds generating as much as 100 kilograms of carbon dioxide per kilogram of meat. Though chocolate and coffee do release high amounts of CO2, these foods have a smaller effect on greenhouse gas emissions given their production volume.

Food Production (Tonnes)

The adjacent time series illustrates the amount of beef and milk produced by the three major contributors of global greenhouse gas emissions. Though India occupies the 3rd largest share of emissions, beef production in India has seldom increased since 1990. This is because most people practise Hinduism in India, which preaches a lacto-vegetarian lifestyle and condemns beef consumption.

Cattle Count vs Cattle Slaughtered

The adjacent pie charts show the number of live cattle versus cattle slaughtered as at 2015. Though India has the 2nd largest cattle count in the world, slaughter rates pail in comparison to Brazil and the U.S.A. Consequently, cows live longer and emit more greenhouse gases in India.

Concluding Remarks

There are ramifications not only in producing beef, but also in raising livestock. Increasingly, we need to develop more sustainable ways of farming so that we can protect the environment from further damage.
